Blue Heron

"Looks like we’ve got time to kill and I’m tired of watching tail-lights. I have a story—the one dream I remember clearly. It starts in the mist just after dawn. I’m walking on a beach leading to the bay, which isn’t the main beach. It’s over the causeway. In the bay the water is typically still. No tides. And with the mist everything’s muffled like when it is snowing rather than fog.  So we can’t hear much beyond our own sand-shuffling.

Drew and I are heading down the bay beach to get to…someplace. I don’t remember where but it doesn’t matter. We’d be walking for awhile, no matter where we had to go. The mist rises and everything is pastels: the cream sand, the sharp blue dune grass, the grey water. The air is golden and bubbly, like champagne. Bits of mist eddy around shadows.  It’s really gorgeous.

We just walk along, looking around in awe. As time passes, something starts to tingle at the back of my mind. Something’s off. I’m looking at the colors, the trees, the water, trying to figure it out but I don’t want to mention it to Drew in case it’s nothing. He looks so peaceful.

We keep walking. I suddenly decide it’s the sounds that are off. At this hour, we should hear the bay waking up around us—bugs, gulls, buoy’s bells as fishermen start up. But it’s completely quiet. Drew’s starting to look uneasy, too.  His smile is tight and small.

I catch his eye and he gives a shaky nod. We know not to speak but can’t say why that’s dangerous. We walk as quietly as we can. I can feel the fear radiating off the dunes and the water seems to lap it back on us, like an ‘I told you so’ jeering.

A breeze silently ruffles the blade of grass I’m frantically staring at and it hits me:

'Oh, shit. Blue heron.'

'RUN!'

I take off. I think I saw Drew go saucer-eyes just before he started sprinting, too. Now we’re making a ton of noise, slamming up the beach as fast as we can run in the sand.

I hear an accelerating high-pitched shriek from overhead and BAM! sand explodes behind us, sending as shower of stuff and dust all over our backs. There’s a concussion of air that flattens the grass and rings the water, pushing us forward an extra step. I hear this nails-on-chalkboard, deranged scream behind me as the heron climbs out of the sand and launches up in to the sky again.  It’s the sound of pure fury.  The dogfight whine picks up again, circling above us as we run. Then in turns for us, getting louder.

BAM!, the heron slams into the sand ahead of me a few paces, knocking us off our route and slowing our pace as we cough and flail at flying particles. The heron’s head emerges from the crater’s mushroom-cloud column of dust and it turns its head perpendicular to me to stare with one unnatural eye.  Hell-heron.  It screams again, louder, targeting me with its sound like a banshee.  Launching itself skyward, it throws a bigger shadow with every wing-beat.  It feels like the sand is cowering, too.

I catch Drew gesturing wildly and we start zig-zagging as we run, giving the heron a moving target.

It screams around above us and we hear the plummet start nearly at the edge of hearing. This one’s gonna be huge.  We’ll never survive.

…

And then I wake up. I’m not joking—that’s exactly how it went. Four words: “Oh, shit, blue heron”.  They’re burned into my memory.  I’m pretty sure once I was fully awake I started laughing.  I never understood where we were going, why or what pissed off the blue heron. Or why Drew. But it’s the most vivid dream I ever had and I *still* get edgy walking that beach.

Yeah, go ahead, keep laughing.  You’ll see.”
