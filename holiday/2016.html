2016 Holiday Letter

2016:  What a Miserable Excuse for a Year 

“Old Man” Moulson passed away this year.  In late March, Moulson suffered a second vestibular attack that impacted his ability to walk, spelling the end.  I spent one more day with him — endless treats, hugs, ear-rubs and such — then our vet and I let him go.  It was very sad but a merciful end for a good dog.  He is missed by many.

Dragon got a pet cat.  A few months after Mo’s passing, Dragon made his discontent known about being the lone pet.  He did so by sitting on me in the middle of the night and meowing questions to which I had no answers.  My interpretation: he needed a harassing figure in his life to enforce a pecking order.  It is too soon for a new dog, so I got him a cat:  a traumatized, adult street-rescue, now named Behemoth.

We don’t know much about Behemoth’s background except he’s very scared, very big and very slowly getting used to us.  He may have been kept as a breeder because he is a rare Selkirk Rex, which is a new natural breed known for a curly hair mutation.  His fur looks like a worn, much-loved stuffed animal.  He’s disinclined to share his tragic backstory, which is fine.  Like Moulson’s painful past, we’ll rehabilitate him without dwelling on things.

Behemoth and Dragon are already firm friends.  They play and cuddle together, although Dragon is a lightweight compared to Behemoth.  Dragon can pounce all he wants but Behemoth just looks bored and irritated, which amuses me to no end.  Moulson would have approved of Behemoth’s approach to life.

Work was tough.  This was year four of the four-year news cycle, the hardest year.  The internet started calling 2016 “the worst year” around the time of Bowie’s passing…on January 10th.  And it just got worse.  As jokesters observed on Twitter, not even Fidel Castro could outlive 2016.  Overall, it was a big year for The NYT.  I spent it focused on our live coverage tools, which were frequently in use between political events and sports, in particular.  

We saw few breaks in the pace:  all the usual yearly stuff, as well as a summer olympics and the bulk of a presidential election culminating in election night.  I even published a rather nerdy story in the newspaper, about scalpers profiting off the hit Broadway musical “Hamilton”.  The Summer Olympics and a General Election are two are big, multi-month efforts for my team, Interactive News.  And we did some great work this year.  Our website and data held up solidly for Election Night, which was a feat that should have made us proud but the election result and its implications for us squashed any interest in back-patting.

It was a ridiculously busy (and dispiriting) news year — let’s not do that again, please.

Family.  Most of my traveling was dedicated to helping with my mom’s declining health yet busy timeshare travel schedule. With my family, I spent some time in Puerto Vallarta, Mexico, where the resort layout works for Mom’s new wheelchair.  We’ll be returning in 2017!

I donated a kidney.  Long story short, a fellow “news nerd” needed a kidney and we were a match, so I donated one of mine in mid-December.  The transplant was successful!  My recovery is ongoing even as edit this letter. 

The proud new owner of my kidney is an acquaintance (now friend), Michelle Minkoff, who suffers an autoimmune disease that attacks her kidneys thereby limiting their lifespan.  Eighteen months ago, her first donated kidney started to fail.  She was a year away from getting on the transplant list and even further from finding a match.  Being a very resourceful and relentlessly positive person, Michelle and friends set up a website and survey to find volunteers willing to be tested as a match.  They promoted it on Twitter and Facebook, hoping to build a roster of candidates.  Myself and around 50 others signed up.  Her medical team was flummoxed by the response, as well as her proactivity.  

As Michelle’s kidney function decreased throughout 2016, her medical support team started thinning the list of volunteers.  A handful of us made the first cut.  Tests confirmed I was her best match, which had felt inevitable to me from the very start anyways.  In early December, I flew to Chicago and the hospital performed the transplant. 

The long-term impact of having just one kidney is surprisingly minimal.  The remaining kidney grows to provide up to 75% of the previous functionality of two.  Post-surgery treatment is Tylenol for pain…and that’s about it.  Until the incisions heal, no lifting “heavy” things. (I can pick up Dragon but not Behemoth, per guidelines.)  Watch excess salt.  Keep a healthy lifestyle, etc.  I’m much healthier than the average donor — I’m definitely grateful for that.

Michelle is doing very well with her recovery, too.  Please send spare karma and good wishes her direction!
