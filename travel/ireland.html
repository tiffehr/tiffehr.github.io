County Tour

Late summer of 2012, my good friends Jaime, Willie and myself traveled to the Ireland-shaped conceptual hole in my map of the world. I spent some time before, during and after the trip ruminating on why Ireland was a vague blankness to me compared to other places I’ve traveled, because I still find it disconcerting.

Me, believing myself to be a cultured traveler yet still amused at playing King of the Rock.

Reviewing my travels without the sentimentality of myself as some cultured traveler…nearly all of them have been blind trips to places I know only in terms of geographic or political-boundary orientation. What I like to think of as knowledge is in fact a decent cartographic education in 7th grade—now showing its geopolitical age—plus vague facts from high school bullet-pointed histories.

And a LOT of desultory concepts gleaned from pop culture, movies and books.  Voila! Presumed above-average knowledge just because I can name all the countries of the mid-’90s political map in a song from “Animaniacs” (not a lie).  

I do wonder if American history teachers assume students know Ireland’s history from Irish-American immigrant caricatures, just because they are a notable pattern in our national history. (I vaguely remember a viewing of “Far and Away” in high school classes…but we also watched “Tombstone” so it’s hard to recall clearly if either were the most resonant piece of a particular era of US history—I really hope they weren’t.)

But it may just be a quirk of my education. For a girl from a Scotch-Irish family (on one side), I ought to have absorbed at least as much context on the Irish side as the Scottish side. But I didn’t. I’m grateful to Ireland for patiently reshaping my concept of the country and people.

A last fit of musing: whatever education crossroads where people turn to reading Irish literature and authors in the larger context of Western literature, I missed that, too. Thankfully, Jaime and Willie had more than enough experience and interest to share. And I have a lengthy list of books by which to catch-up for next visit.

Dublin

We naturally started in Dublin. More specifically, a tour of Dublin’s pubs led by a local musician and new-found friend. If you believe in single events setting a tenor for a trip, ours was set by our Guinness-fueled, lengthy conversations with local-compatriot JP about books, linguistics and the great vowel shift, Irish history and music as we journeyed pub to pub. It was a hell of a precedent to set.
